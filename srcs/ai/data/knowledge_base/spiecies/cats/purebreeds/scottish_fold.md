# Scottish Fold

## Overview
The Scottish Fold originated from a spontaneous dominant genetic mutation discovered on a Scottish farm in 1961. Their signature folded ears (caused by a cartilage defect) give them a distinctive owl-like or teddy-bear appearance. Despite their popularity — amplified globally by celebrity ownership — Scottish Folds are subject to significant veterinary controversy due to inherited health issues associated with the fold gene (Fd). The UK's Governing Council of the Cat Fancy (GCCF) banned registration of new Scottish Folds in 1974 due to welfare concerns.

## Temperament
- **Gentle**: Sweet-natured and adaptable; adjusts well to most household environments
- **Playful**: Enjoys interactive play and can be trained to play fetch
- **Social**: Gets along well with other pets and children; not aggressive
- **Calm**: Not overly demanding or vocal; comfortable with moderate alone time
- **Inquisitive**: Alert and curious; enjoys watching household activities
- **Characteristic Posture**: Often sits in a "Buddha pose" (sitting upright with belly exposed) or lies stretched out flat

## Physical Characteristics
- **Size**: Medium (6–13 lbs)
- **Build**: Medium-sized, well-rounded body with a round head and large round eyes
- **Ear Types**:
  - **Folded** (Fd gene present): Ears fold forward and downward — single fold (tips forward), double fold (closer to head), triple fold (ears lie flat)
  - **Straight-eared Scottish Fold** (Highland Straight if long-haired): Essential for healthy breeding programs; one parent must be straight-eared
- **Coat**: Short and plush (Scottish Fold) or semi-long (Highland Fold / Scottish Fold Longhair)
- **Colors**: Wide variety — white, black, blue, cream, tabby, bicolor, tortoiseshell
- **Eyes**: Large, round, wide-set; can be any color matching coat pattern
- **Lifespan**: 11–14 years (may be shorter due to health complications)

## Health Considerations
- **Osteochondrodysplasia (OCD)**: The fold gene (Fd) in homozygous form (two copies, Fd/Fd) causes severe generalized skeletal disease affecting all cartilage — painful, progressive, and untreatable. Even heterozygous folds (Fd/fd) carry risk of degenerative joint disease
- **Painful Arthritis**: Folded-ear cats frequently develop painful degenerative joint disease, particularly in the tail, ankles, and knees; often underdiagnosed due to cats masking pain
- **Heart Disease (HCM)**: Elevated risk compared to mixed-breed cats
- **Ear Infections**: The folded ear canal traps moisture and debris; regular gentle cleaning is essential
- **Pain Assessment**: Scottish Folds often do not vocalize pain — watch for reluctance to jump, stiff gait, reduced activity, or resistance to being touched
- **Ethical Note**: Many veterinary organizations including the British Veterinary Association advise against breeding or purchasing fold-eared cats due to unavoidable suffering

## Care Requirements
- **Veterinary Attention**: Regular orthopedic assessment; early X-rays of joints recommended; baseline evaluation before symptoms appear
- **Pain Management**: Work with a veterinarian to assess and manage chronic pain if present; NSAIDs or other pain relief as prescribed
- **Ear Care**: Gentle weekly cleaning of the ear canals; folded structure traps debris — do not probe deeply
- **Exercise**: Encourage gentle movement but avoid high-impact activities; ramps and low cat trees preferred over tall climbing structures
- **Grooming**: Weekly brushing for shorthair; more frequent for longhair variety
- **Diet**: Balanced diet; maintain healthy weight to reduce joint stress
- **Responsible Sourcing**: Fold × fold breedings should be avoided; fold × straight is the only breeding pairing recommended for welfare reasons
