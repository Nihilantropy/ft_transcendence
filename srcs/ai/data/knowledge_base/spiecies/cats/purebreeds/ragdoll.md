# Ragdoll

## Overview
The Ragdoll was developed by breeder Ann Baker in California in the 1960s by selectively breeding non-pedigree domestic cats. Named for their tendency to go limp and relaxed when picked up, Ragdolls are known as the ultimate "gentle giant" of the cat world. They are consistently among the top three most popular cat breeds globally and are prized for their docile, loving nature and semi-long silky coats.

## Temperament
- **Docile**: Exceptionally gentle and relaxed; goes limp when held (the "ragdoll" trait)
- **Affectionate**: Deeply loving; follows owners around and seeks physical contact
- **Social**: Greets owners at the door; enjoys being held and cuddled more than most cats
- **Calm**: Low-key temperament; rarely aggressive; tolerates handling well
- **Child-Friendly**: Among the best cat breeds for families with young children
- **Indoor-Oriented**: Not street-savvy; low self-preservation instinct makes outdoor exposure dangerous

## Physical Characteristics
- **Size**: Large to extra-large (females 10–15 lbs; males 15–20 lbs); slow to mature, reaching full size at 3–4 years
- **Body**: Long, muscular, with a broad chest and heavy boning; feels heavier than they look
- **Coat**: Semi-long, silky, low-mat coat with a soft rabbit-like texture; minimal undercoat compared to Maine Coon or Norwegian Forest Cat
- **Pattern**: Always color-pointed (darker extremities) in one of three patterns:
  - **Colorpoint**: Dark points, no white
  - **Mitted**: Dark points with white paws and belly stripe
  - **Bicolor**: Inverted "V" white blaze on face, white legs and belly
- **Colors**: Seal, blue, chocolate, lilac, red, cream, and tortie variations
- **Eyes**: Always blue, oval-shaped — deeper blue is more prized
- **Lifespan**: 12–17 years

## Health Considerations
- **Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM)**: Ragdolls carry a specific mutation (MYBPC3 R820W) associated with HCM; genetic testing available and strongly recommended for breeding cats; annual echocardiograms advised
- **Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD)**: Some bloodlines affected; DNA test available
- **Feline Infectious Peritonitis (FIP)**: Ragdolls appear to have higher susceptibility; keep vaccinations current and avoid multi-cat shelter environments
- **Obesity**: Calm temperament and indoor lifestyle — weight management is essential throughout life
- **Urinary Tract Issues**: Ensure adequate hydration; wet food and water fountains recommended
- **Bladder Stones**: Monitor for signs of urinary discomfort

## Care Requirements
- **Grooming**: Brushing 2–3 times weekly; the coat has minimal matting tendency but benefits from regular maintenance; pay attention to the belly and collar area
- **Exercise**: Moderate activity level; enjoys interactive play but is not as demanding as Bengal or Siamese; daily sessions recommended to prevent obesity
- **Social Needs**: Thrives with human companionship; can suffer from loneliness — best in households with people home frequently, or paired with another Ragdoll
- **Safety**: Must be kept strictly indoors; their trusting, fearless nature makes them vulnerable outdoors
- **Diet**: High-quality protein diet with measured portions; avoid free-feeding
- **Cardiac Screening**: Recommend annual echocardiogram after age 2, especially for breeding cats
