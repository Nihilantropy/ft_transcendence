# Abyssinian

## Overview
The Abyssinian is one of the oldest known cat breeds, with origins still debated — likely from coastal areas of the Indian Ocean and Southeast Asia rather than ancient Abyssinia (modern Ethiopia) as traditionally claimed. DNA studies suggest proximity to cats from the Bay of Bengal region. They are renowned for their distinctive ticked tabby coat (each hair banded with multiple colors), lithe athletic body, and boundless energy. Abyssinians consistently rank among the world's most popular cat breeds.

## Temperament
- **Highly Active**: One of the most energetic cat breeds; constantly moving, climbing, and exploring
- **Curious**: Investigates every sound, movement, and new object in the environment
- **Intelligent**: Quick learners; can be trained to walk on a leash, fetch, and perform tricks
- **Social**: Enjoy human company and may follow owners around but are not typically lap cats
- **Independent-Minded**: Affectionate on their own terms; not clingy or overly dependent
- **Playful**: Remain active and playful well into old age; kitten-like throughout their lives
- **Alert**: Highly observant; excellent at noticing environmental changes

## Physical Characteristics
- **Size**: Small to medium (6–10 lbs); fine-boned and elegant
- **Build**: Slender, lithe, muscular body — considered the "ideal" athletic cat form; resembling ancient Egyptian cat depictions
- **Coat**: Short, dense, fine, with distinctive **ticked tabby pattern** (agouti ticking — each hair has 2–3 bands of alternating light and dark color)
- **Colors**: Ruddy (warm brown/orange with black ticking) is classic; also cinnamon (warm reddish-brown), blue (warm grey with deeper grey ticking), fawn (warm cream with light brown ticking)
- **Ears**: Large, broad-based, alert, moderately pointed — giving a perpetually alert expression
- **Eyes**: Large, almond-shaped, expressive; gold, green, copper, or hazel — outlined by dark "kohl" marking
- **Tail**: Moderately long, tapering to a dark tip
- **Lifespan**: 12–15 years

## Health Considerations
- **Progressive Retinal Atrophy (PRA)**: High prevalence in Abyssinians due to a specific mutation (rdAc-PRA); causes progressive blindness; DNA testing critical for responsible breeding
- **Pyruvate Kinase Deficiency (PK Deficiency)**: Genetic enzyme deficiency causing hemolytic anemia; DNA test available; responsible breeders screen all cats
- **Renal Amyloidosis**: Abnormal protein deposits in kidneys leading to kidney failure; familial form documented in Abyssinians; more common than in most breeds
- **Dental Disease**: Prone to gingivitis and periodontitis; regular dental care essential
- **Hyperthyroidism**: Higher prevalence in older Abyssinians than in general cat population
- **Patellar Luxation**: Kneecap dislocation; monitor for abnormal gait or leg shaking

## Care Requirements
- **Exercise**: Requires 30–60+ minutes of active play daily; thrives with environmental enrichment (tall cat trees, wall shelves, outdoor enclosures/catios)
- **Mental Stimulation**: Puzzle feeders, clicker training, interactive toys; an unstimulated Abyssinian will find entertainment at owner's expense
- **Space**: Not well-suited to very small apartments without vertical territory
- **Leash Training**: Takes well to harness and leash walks; enjoy outdoor exploration under supervision
- **Grooming**: Minimal — weekly brushing sufficient due to short, ticked coat; low shedding
- **Diet**: High-quality protein diet; moderate caloric needs; active metabolism
- **Companionship**: Pairs well with another Abyssinian or an equally active breed; does not thrive in solitary confinement
- **Genetic Testing**: Before purchasing from a breeder, confirm PRA and PK-Def testing of both parents
