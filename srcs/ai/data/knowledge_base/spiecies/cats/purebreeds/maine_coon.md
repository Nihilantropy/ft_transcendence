# Maine Coon

## Overview
The Maine Coon is America's largest domestic cat breed and one of the oldest natural breeds in North America. Originating in the state of Maine, they are nicknamed "the dogs of the cat world" for their playful, loyal, and interactive personalities. They hold the Guinness World Record for the longest domestic cat. Despite their wild appearance, Maine Coons are gentle giants known for their sociability.

## Temperament
- **Playful**: Retains kitten-like playfulness well into adulthood; loves interactive toys and fetch
- **Intelligent**: Strong problem-solving ability; responds well to clicker training and learning tricks
- **Social**: Excellent with children, other cats, and cat-friendly dogs; genuinely enjoys company
- **Loyal**: Follows owners from room to room; described as dog-like in attachment
- **Talkative**: Communicates with a characteristic repertoire of chirps, trills, and soft chattering rather than typical meows

## Physical Characteristics
- **Size**: Large to extra-large (females 8–12 lbs; males 13–25 lbs); slow to mature, reaching full size at 3–5 years
- **Coat**: Thick, shaggy, water-repellent semi-long coat; shorter on the shoulders, flowing on the belly and flanks; uneven and natural-looking
- **Ruff**: Distinctive lion-like ruff around the neck; more prominent in males
- **Tail**: Long, bushy tail — often compared in appearance to a raccoon tail
- **Ears**: Large, tufted ears with prominent lynx-like ear tips
- **Paws**: Large, round, heavily tufted paws adapted for walking on snow
- **Colors**: Over 75 color combinations — tabby patterns most iconic, but also solid, tortoiseshell, and bicolor
- **Lifespan**: 12–15 years

## Health Considerations
- **Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM)**: Most common cardiac disease in cats; Maine Coons carry a breed-specific mutation (MyBPC3 A31P) that significantly increases risk — DNA testing available and recommended for all breeding cats
- **Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA)**: Autosomal recessive genetic condition causing hind limb muscle wasting; not painful but progressive; DNA test available
- **Hip Dysplasia**: Larger frame increases susceptibility; monitor for reluctance to jump or hind limb lameness
- **Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD)**: Less prevalent than in Persians but possible
- **Obesity**: Large appetite combined with indoor lifestyle; monitor body condition regularly
- **Joint Issues**: Significant body mass stresses joints with age; weight management is crucial

## Care Requirements
- **Grooming**: Brushing 2–3 times weekly; the coat is relatively mat-resistant compared to Persians but requires consistent maintenance; pay attention to belly and armpit areas
- **Exercise**: High energy for a cat; requires interactive play sessions daily and tall, sturdy climbing structures (large cat trees essential)
- **Space**: Benefits from generous indoor space; needs vertical territory as well as horizontal
- **Mental Stimulation**: Puzzle feeders, fetch games, wand toys, and clicker training prevent boredom
- **Diet**: High-quality protein-rich diet; large-breed formulas appropriate; measure portions to prevent obesity
- **Water**: Many Maine Coons are fascinated by running water; a pet water fountain encourages hydration (beneficial for kidney health)
- **Cardiac Screening**: Annual echocardiograms recommended for breeding cats and individuals with cardiac risk
