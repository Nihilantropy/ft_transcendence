# Bengal

## Overview
The Bengal is a hybrid breed created by crossing domestic cats with the Asian Leopard Cat (Prionailurus bengalensis), first developed by geneticist Jean Mill in the 1970s and 1980s. Their striking wild appearance — featuring vivid spotted or marbled coats with a glittery sheen — combined with a domestic temperament make them highly sought after. Bengals require significant stimulation and are not suited for sedentary households.

## Temperament
- **Highly Active**: Among the most energetic domestic cat breeds; needs extensive daily exercise
- **Athletic**: Natural climbers and leapers; can jump to surprising heights
- **Intelligent**: Problem-solvers that learn quickly; can open doors, drawers, and latches
- **Curious**: Investigates everything; attracted to water unlike most cats (enjoy playing in sinks and showers)
- **Demanding**: Vocal and insistent when bored or understimulated; requires significant owner engagement
- **Confident**: Bold, assertive, and self-assured; not for owners who prefer passive cats

## Physical Characteristics
- **Size**: Medium to large (8–15 lbs)
- **Build**: Long, muscular, athletic body with a broad head; males notably larger than females
- **Coat**: Short to medium, incredibly soft and silky with a distinctive wild "glitter" effect (light-reflective sheen)
- **Pattern**: Spotted (randomly spotted or rosette — two-toned spots like a leopard) or marbled (flowing horizontal swirls); high contrast pattern on lighter background
- **Colors**: Brown/black spotted tabby most common; also snow (seal lynx point, seal mink, seal sepia), silver, blue, and charcoal
- **Rosetting**: Higher-generation Bengals have open or closed rosettes similar to ocelots or leopards rather than simple spots
- **Eyes**: Large, oval eyes in green, gold, hazel, or blue (snow variants)
- **Lifespan**: 12–16 years

## Health Considerations
- **Progressive Retinal Atrophy (PRA-b)**: Bengal-specific genetic mutation causing blindness; DNA testing available and critical for responsible breeding
- **Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM)**: Elevated risk in Bengals; annual cardiac screening recommended
- **Tritrichomonas Foetus**: Intestinal parasite causing chronic diarrhea; more prevalent in Bengals — fecal testing recommended
- **Cataracts**: Elevated incidence in some lines; regular eye examinations advisable
- **Flat-Chested Kitten Syndrome (FCKS)**: Structural abnormality seen more in Bengals; affects some kittens shortly after birth
- **Behavioral Issues from Understimulation**: Not a physical condition, but chronic boredom leads to destructive behavior, aggression, and anxiety

## Care Requirements
- **Exercise**: Requires 1–2 hours of active play daily; cat wheels, outdoor enclosures (catios), leash training, and fetch games are appropriate
- **Mental Stimulation**: Puzzle feeders, rotating toys, training sessions — Bengals that get bored become destructive
- **Enrichment**: Large cat trees, wall-mounted shelves, bird feeders visible through windows, interactive toys
- **Water Play**: Many Bengals enjoy water; provide a pet fountain; expect water bowl splashing
- **Grooming**: Minimal — weekly brushing sufficient; coat is low-shedding and self-maintaining
- **Leash Training**: Many Bengals adapt well to harness and leash for outdoor exploration under supervision
- **Diet**: High-protein diet reflecting ancestral carnivore needs; raw or high-meat wet food diets are popular among Bengal owners
- **Not Ideal For**: First-time cat owners, apartment dwellers without enrichment plans, households wanting a calm lap cat
