# Sphynx

## Overview
The Sphynx (also spelled Sphinx) is a hairless cat breed that originated from a natural mutation first discovered in Toronto, Canada in 1966. Despite their alien appearance, Sphynx cats are warm to the touch, social, and intensely affectionate. Their lack of fur is due to a recessive mutation in the KRT71 gene. They are not truly bald — they have a fine, soft peach-fuzz down and prominent whiskers that may be curled or absent. The Sphynx is the foundation breed for several other hairless varieties including the Don Sphynx and Peterbald.

## Temperament
- **Extroverted**: Among the most sociable and outgoing of all cat breeds; greets strangers readily
- **Affectionate**: Seeks constant physical contact; loves to snuggle under blankets or against people for warmth
- **Playful**: Highly energetic and clownish; maintains playful nature throughout adulthood
- **Intelligent**: Curious and engaged; learns quickly; responds to training
- **Dog-Like**: Follows owners everywhere; often compared to a dog in behavior and loyalty
- **Demanding**: Not suited for owners who want a cat that gives them space; becomes lonely and distressed when ignored

## Physical Characteristics
- **Size**: Medium (6–12 lbs)
- **Build**: Medium-boned, muscular with a rounded belly (even well-fed) and a pot-belly appearance; deceptively sturdy
- **Skin**: Wrinkled, especially on the head, neck, shoulders, and between the ears; feels like warm suede or chamois leather
- **"Hairless"**: Fine peach-like down may be present, especially on the ears, nose, and tail; not truly bald
- **Ears**: Very large, wide, bat-like ears with no inner ear hair
- **Eyes**: Large, lemon-shaped (slightly angled), striking; any color including odd-eyed
- **Whiskers**: May be entirely absent, broken, or short and sparse
- **Skin Colors/Patterns**: The skin shows pigmentation corresponding to what fur color would be — can be solid, tabby, tortie, bicolor patterns visible in skin
- **Lifespan**: 9–15 years

## Health Considerations
- **Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM)**: Highest HCM prevalence of any cat breed (studies suggest 30–40% of Sphynx develop HCM); annual echocardiograms from age 1 are strongly recommended; genetic testing available but does not capture all mutations
- **Hereditary Myopathy (HCM-related muscle weakness)**: Some Sphynx lines carry additional muscular disorders
- **Sunburn**: Completely unprotected skin sunburns easily — limit direct sun exposure; pet-safe sunscreen available for outdoor access
- **Temperature Regulation**: Cannot retain body heat; require warm environments (above 20°C/68°F minimum), heated beds, and clothing in cold climates
- **Skin Infections**: Oil accumulation in skin folds can cause bacterial or fungal dermatitis; regular bathing essential
- **Ear Wax Buildup**: Excessive accumulation without ear hair to filter debris; weekly cleaning required
- **Respiratory Infections**: More susceptible to upper respiratory infections; monitor carefully in cold or drafty environments
- **Obesity Risk**: Elevated metabolism requires more calories but body condition must be monitored

## Care Requirements
- **Bathing**: Weekly bath is essential to remove natural skin oils that accumulate without fur to distribute them; mild, hypoallergenic cat shampoo
- **Ear Cleaning**: Weekly cleaning with a gentle ear cleaner; dark brown wax accumulates rapidly
- **Warmth**: Provide heated beds, cozy sweaters/wraps for cold environments; they will seek the warmest spots in any home
- **Sun Protection**: Avoid prolonged direct sunlight; indoor lifestyle preferred
- **Exercise**: High energy; requires daily interactive play; enjoys climbing, chasing, and interactive toys
- **Companionship**: Strongly benefits from having another cat (ideally another Sphynx); does not cope well with extended solitude
- **Diet**: Slightly higher caloric needs than furred cats due to heat maintenance; high-quality protein diet
- **Cardiac Monitoring**: Annual echocardiogram starting from 1–2 years of age; discuss with a veterinary cardiologist
