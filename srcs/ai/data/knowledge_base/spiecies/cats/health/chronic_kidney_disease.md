# Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) in Cats

## Overview
Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD), also called Chronic Renal Failure (CRF), is the most common disease of older cats and a leading cause of feline mortality. The kidneys progressively lose their ability to filter waste products, regulate fluid balance, and produce hormones. Because kidneys have significant functional reserve, cats often show no clinical signs until 65–75% of kidney function has been lost. CKD is incurable but manageable — early detection and appropriate nutrition can significantly extend quality of life.

## Symptoms
Symptoms typically appear gradually as disease progresses:

- **Increased thirst (polydipsia)**: One of the earliest signs — drinking much more than usual
- **Increased urination (polyuria)**: Dilute, pale urine; larger clumps in litter box
- **Weight loss**: Progressive muscle wasting and loss of body condition
- **Reduced appetite or anorexia**: Nausea from waste product accumulation
- **Lethargy**: Reduced energy and activity
- **Vomiting**: Uremic toxins cause nausea; may vomit bile or undigested food
- **Bad breath (uremic breath)**: Ammonia-like or fishy odor from waste product buildup
- **Dehydration**: Poor skin turgor; dry, tacky gums
- **Mouth ulcers**: From uremia (toxic waste buildup)
- **Coat deterioration**: Unkempt, dull coat — cat stops grooming effectively
- **Hypertension**: High blood pressure secondary to CKD can cause sudden blindness

## Risk Factors
- **Age**: Most common in cats over 7 years; prevalence rises sharply after age 10
- **Genetics**: Persian and Abyssinian cats predisposed to renal amyloidosis; PKD in Persians causes cystic kidney damage
- **Dehydration**: Cats naturally have low thirst drive; dry food-only diets may contribute to chronic mild dehydration
- **Dental Disease**: Chronic bacterial exposure from periodontal disease may damage kidneys over time
- **Toxin exposure**: Lilies (nephrotoxic plant), antifreeze, certain medications, NSAIDs
- **Previous acute kidney injury**: Healed acute injury may leave reduced functional tissue
- **Hyperthyroidism**: Can mask CKD; treating hyperthyroidism may "unmask" existing CKD

## Prevention Strategies
- **Annual bloodwork**: Blood urea nitrogen (BUN), creatinine, symmetric dimethylarginine (SDMA) after age 7; SDMA detects CKD 17 months earlier than creatinine on average
- **Hydration**: Encourage water intake — water fountains, wet food, adding water to food
- **Toxin prevention**: Keep lilies (all parts toxic), antifreeze, and human medications out of reach
- **Dental care**: Regular dental cleanings reduce systemic bacterial load
- **Urinalysis**: Annual urine specific gravity and protein testing alongside bloodwork
- **PKD/amyloidosis testing**: DNA testing for predisposed breeds

## Treatment Options
CKD is staged by IRIS (International Renal Interest Society) from Stage 1 (early) to Stage 4 (severe):

- **Dietary management (cornerstone of treatment)**: Prescription renal diet — reduced phosphorus, controlled protein, added omega-3 fatty acids, potassium supplementation; renal diets shown to significantly extend survival
- **Hydration support**: Subcutaneous fluids administered at home (owners can learn this); IV fluids during hospitalization
- **Phosphate binders**: Bind dietary phosphorus in the gut (aluminum hydroxide, calcium carbonate, lanthanum carbonate)
- **Potassium supplementation**: CKD cats often become hypokalemic (low potassium)
- **Anti-nausea medication**: Maropitant, ondansetron to improve appetite
- **Appetite stimulants**: Mirtazapine (also available as transdermal ear gel)
- **Antihypertensives**: Amlodipine for secondary hypertension; prevents blindness and further kidney damage
- **Erythropoietin (EPO) analogues**: For non-regenerative anemia in advanced CKD
- **ACE inhibitors**: For proteinuria management in some stages
- **Benazepril**: May slow progression and reduce proteinuria

## Breeds Commonly Affected
- Persian (especially PKD-related CKD)
- Abyssinian (renal amyloidosis)
- Siamese (amyloidosis risk)
- All breeds: general CKD incidence rises with age in all cats
- Domestic shorthair/mixed breed cats are equally susceptible at older ages

## Prognosis
- **Stage 1–2**: Many cats live years with appropriate management; quality of life can be excellent
- **Stage 3**: Median survival 1.86 years with treatment
- **Stage 4**: Median survival months; focus shifts to palliative comfort care
- **Early detection is the single most important factor** in extending survival with good quality of life
- Home subcutaneous fluids and dietary management are the most impactful owner-administered interventions
