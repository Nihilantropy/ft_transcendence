# Hyperthyroidism in Cats

## Overview
Hyperthyroidism is the most common endocrine (hormonal) disorder in cats, particularly those over 10 years of age. It is caused by the overproduction of thyroid hormones (T3 and T4) by one or both thyroid glands, usually due to a benign adenoma (non-cancerous enlargement). Hyperthyroidism accelerates the body's metabolism, leading to weight loss despite increased appetite, and places excessive strain on the heart and kidneys. It is highly treatable, and with appropriate management, affected cats can maintain excellent quality of life for years.

## Symptoms
Symptoms develop gradually and may initially be mistaken for normal aging:

- **Weight loss**: Dramatic weight loss despite increased or ravenous appetite (polyphagia) — the hallmark sign
- **Increased appetite (polyphagia)**: Often the first change owners notice; cat seems "always hungry"
- **Hyperactivity or restlessness**: Especially in older cats that were previously calm; increased vocalization at night
- **Increased thirst and urination**: Secondary to elevated metabolic rate
- **Vomiting and diarrhea**: Especially large-volume, undigested food; chronic gastrointestinal upset
- **Coat deterioration**: Unkempt, greasy, or matted coat; cat stops self-grooming effectively
- **Rapid heart rate (tachycardia)**: Palpable even at rest; heart murmur may develop
- **High blood pressure (hypertension)**: Can cause sudden blindness from retinal detachment
- **Enlarged thyroid gland**: Palpable lump(s) in the neck — detectable by a veterinarian
- **Muscle wasting**: Loss of muscle mass despite high food intake
- **Weakness or reluctance to jump**

## Risk Factors
- **Age**: Primarily affects cats over 10 years; peak incidence at 12–13 years
- **Sex**: Affects males and females equally; neutering status not a significant factor
- **Diet**: Possible association with certain dietary factors; higher incidence in cats fed exclusively canned food containing fish or liver flavors (proposed iodine or endocrine disruptor connection — research ongoing)
- **Environmental factors**: Possible link to flame retardant chemicals (PBDEs) in household dust; cats groom ingested particles from fur
- **Indoor lifestyle**: Indoor cats may be more exposed to household chemicals
- **Breed**: Himalayan and Siamese cats appear to have lower-than-average risk; domestic cats at standard population risk

## Prevention Strategies
- **Annual thyroid screening after age 7–8**: T4 measurement in blood panels; adds minimal cost to routine bloodwork
- **Reduce flame retardant exposure**: Where possible, opt for furniture with minimal PBDE content; vacuum frequently
- **Regular veterinary geriatric checkups**: Annual bloodwork including kidney, thyroid, and blood pressure assessment for cats over 7 years

## Treatment Options
Hyperthyroidism is one of the most treatable serious feline diseases. Four treatment options are available:

**1. Medical Management (Methimazole / Felimazole):**
- Daily oral medication or transdermal ear gel (applied to inner ear flap)
- Controls thyroid hormone production (does not cure)
- Requires lifelong administration and regular monitoring
- Blood and kidney checks every 3–6 months
- Possible side effects: vomiting, facial itching, liver disease, blood cell disorders (uncommon)
- Most accessible and immediately reversible option

**2. Radioactive Iodine (I-131) — Gold Standard:**
- Single injection of radioactive iodine destroys overactive thyroid tissue
- 95%+ cure rate with a single treatment; permanent resolution
- Requires brief hospitalization (3–10 days) until radiation levels are safe
- Most cost-effective long-term solution
- Slightly higher upfront cost but no ongoing medication expenses
- Not available in all locations; referral to specialist center required

**3. Surgical Thyroidectomy:**
- Surgical removal of one or both thyroid glands
- Effective; cure rate ~90%
- Carries anesthetic risk in older cats
- Hypoparathyroidism risk if parathyroid glands are damaged during surgery
- Less commonly chosen than radioactive iodine

**4. Dietary Management (y/d Prescription Diet):**
- Iodine-restricted food that limits thyroid hormone production
- Effective only if the cat eats exclusively this food (treats, water, outdoor hunting must be eliminated)
- Requires strict dietary compliance
- Less effective for multi-cat households; reversible if diet is discontinued

## Important: CKD Masking
- **Hyperthyroidism masks chronic kidney disease** — elevated blood pressure from hyperthyroidism artificially maintains kidney filtration rate
- Treating hyperthyroidism can "unmask" underlying CKD that was previously hidden
- Methimazole can be used as a reversible test: treat for 4–6 weeks then reassess kidney function before committing to radioactive iodine or surgery
- Monitoring kidney values closely after treatment initiation is essential

## Breeds Commonly Affected
- Domestic shorthair and longhair cats (highest absolute numbers — most common cat type)
- Persian: Lower risk than average
- Siamese and Himalayan: Lower risk than average
- All non-Siamese/Himalayan breeds at similar risk
- No specific pedigree breed at dramatically elevated risk — this is primarily an age-related disease

## Prognosis
- **With treatment**: Excellent — most cats respond well and return to near-normal weight and activity
- **Radioactive iodine success**: 95–98% cure rate; many cats live 3–5+ more quality years after treatment
- **Medical management**: Good quality of life achievable with consistent monitoring
- **Untreated**: Progressive cardiac disease (thyrotoxic cardiomyopathy), hypertension, blindness, and eventual heart failure; life-threatening
- **Combined CKD + hyperthyroidism**: More complex management; prognosis depends on severity of both conditions
