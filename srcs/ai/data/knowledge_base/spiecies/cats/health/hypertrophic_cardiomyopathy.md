# Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM) in Cats

## Overview
Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM) is the most common heart disease in cats, affecting an estimated 10–15% of all domestic cats to some degree. It is characterized by the abnormal thickening (hypertrophy) of the left ventricular wall of the heart, which reduces the heart's ability to fill properly and pump blood efficiently. HCM can remain asymptomatic for years before progressing to heart failure, arterial thromboembolism, or sudden death. It is a leading cause of death in cats under 5 years old in certain breeds.

## Symptoms
HCM is often called the "silent killer" because many cats show no symptoms until the disease is advanced:

- **Early/Subclinical (often none)**: Cat appears healthy; HCM detectable only by echocardiogram
- **Breathing difficulties**: Labored breathing, open-mouth breathing, rapid respiratory rate (>30 breaths/minute at rest is a warning sign)
- **Lethargy**: Unusual tiredness or reduced activity
- **Reduced appetite or weight loss**
- **Heart murmur**: Detected during veterinary examination (not always present with HCM)
- **Gallop rhythm**: Abnormal heart sound detectable by stethoscope
- **Aortic Thromboembolism (ATE)**: Sudden hind limb paralysis, severe pain, cold limbs — a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate veterinary care
- **Sudden death**: In severe cases, especially in young cats

## Risk Factors
- **Breed predisposition**: Certain breeds have far higher rates than the general population (see below)
- **Genetic mutations**: MYBPC3 mutations (A31P in Maine Coons; R820W in Ragdolls) confirmed; many other HCM-causing mutations remain unidentified
- **Age**: Risk increases with age in both sexes; breed-specific HCM often appears earlier (young adult onset in Maine Coons and Sphynx)
- **Sex**: Intact males and neutered males often affected more severely than females
- **Genetics**: Autosomal dominant with variable penetrance — a cat carrying the gene may or may not develop clinical disease

## Prevention Strategies
- **Genetic testing**: DNA tests available for Maine Coon (MyBPC3 A31P) and Ragdoll (MyBPC3 R820W) — responsible breeders test all breeding cats
- **Echocardiographic screening**: Annual or biennial cardiac ultrasound screening recommended for at-risk breeds — the only reliable way to detect HCM before symptoms appear
- **Reputable breeders**: Purchase from breeders who provide proof of HCM screening for both parents
- **Regular veterinary checkups**: Annual physical examination including cardiac auscultation; chest X-ray if murmur detected
- **Respiratory Rate Monitoring**: Owners can learn to measure resting respiratory rate at home — >30 breaths/minute warrants veterinary contact

## Treatment Options
HCM is managed, not cured. Treatment aims to slow progression and prevent complications:
- **Atenolol or other beta-blockers**: Reduce heart rate and improve diastolic filling
- **Diltiazem (calcium channel blocker)**: Relaxes heart muscle, improves filling
- **Diuretics (furosemide)**: Manage fluid accumulation in lungs (congestive heart failure)
- **Antithrombotic therapy (clopidogrel, aspirin)**: Reduces risk of blood clots (ATE)
- **Cardiologist-directed monitoring**: Regular follow-up echocardiograms to track progression
- **Restricted activity**: Reduce stress on the heart during symptomatic phases

## Breeds Commonly Affected
- **Maine Coon** (MyBPC3 A31P mutation — 35% carrier rate in some population studies)
- **Ragdoll** (MyBPC3 R820W mutation)
- **Sphynx** (30–40% prevalence; specific mutation not fully characterized)
- **British Shorthair** (elevated prevalence)
- **Norwegian Forest Cat** (elevated prevalence)
- **Birman**
- **Persian** (moderate prevalence)
- **Domestic Shorthair/Mixed breed cats** (HCM also affects non-pedigree cats)

## Prognosis
Prognosis is highly variable:
- **Subclinical cats** with no symptoms may live years without progression
- **Cats with congestive heart failure**: Median survival 6–18 months with treatment
- **Cats experiencing ATE**: Poor short-term prognosis; 50% die within the first episode; survivors often progress to heart failure
- **Early detection significantly improves outcomes** by allowing treatment before crisis

## Emergency Protocol
**Aortic Thromboembolism (ATE)** is a medical emergency:
- Sudden hind limb weakness or paralysis
- Cold, pale, or bluish limb pads
- Vocalization from pain
- Action: Rush to emergency veterinary hospital immediately — do not wait
- Do NOT massage or warm the limbs at home — this can cause reperfusion injury
