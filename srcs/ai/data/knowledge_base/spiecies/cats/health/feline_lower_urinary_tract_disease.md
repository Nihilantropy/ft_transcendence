# Feline Lower Urinary Tract Disease (FLUTD)

## Overview
Feline Lower Urinary Tract Disease (FLUTD) is an umbrella term for a group of conditions affecting the bladder and urethra of cats. It is one of the most common reasons cats are presented to veterinarians, affecting 1–3% of cats annually. Causes include feline idiopathic cystitis (FIC, the most common cause at ~55–65% of cases), urinary stones (uroliths), urethral plugs, bacterial infections, and anatomical abnormalities. Male cats face significantly higher risk of life-threatening urethral obstruction due to their narrower urethra — this is a veterinary emergency.

## Symptoms
- **Straining to urinate (dysuria)**: Crouching in litter box without producing urine, or passing only small amounts
- **Frequent urination attempts**: Multiple litter box visits with minimal output
- **Blood in urine (hematuria)**: Pink, red, or brown-tinged urine
- **Painful urination (stranguria)**: Crying or vocalizing while attempting to urinate
- **Urinating outside the litter box**: Including on cool surfaces like tile or bathtubs
- **Excessive licking of genital area**
- **Lethargy and loss of appetite** (especially with obstruction)
- **Vomiting** (especially with urethral blockage — systemic toxicity)
- **Complete absence of urination** (EMERGENCY — see below)

## Risk Factors
- **Sex**: Male cats at much higher risk of urethral obstruction due to narrower urethra
- **Age**: Middle-aged cats (2–6 years) most commonly affected for FIC; older cats more prone to urinary stones
- **Neutered males**: Higher risk of obstruction than intact males (narrower urethra)
- **Indoor lifestyle**: Sedentary behavior, multiple cats causing stress
- **Stress**: Major trigger for FIC — changes in routine, new pets, household conflict, moving
- **Dry food diet**: Low moisture intake concentrates urine and promotes crystal formation
- **Obesity**: Risk factor for FIC and urinary stones
- **Multi-cat households**: Social tension increases FIC episodes
- **Breed**: Persian cats predisposed to calcium oxalate stones; Himalayan also at elevated risk

## Prevention Strategies
- **Increase water intake**: Wet food (contains 70–80% moisture vs. 10% in dry food); water fountains; multiple water stations
- **Stress reduction**: Feliway diffusers (synthetic pheromone); hiding spots; vertical territory; reducing inter-cat conflict; predictable routines
- **Environmental enrichment**: Play sessions, scratching posts, window perches — reduce boredom and stress
- **Diet**: Prescription urinary diets for cats with recurrent stones or plugs (controls pH and mineral content)
- **Regular litter box maintenance**: Clean, accessible litter boxes in quiet locations; 1 box per cat + 1 extra; avoid covered boxes for stressed cats
- **Weight management**: Prevent obesity
- **Purine-controlled diet**: For cats with urate stones
- **Monitor urination**: Track frequency, volume, and color regularly

## Treatment Options
Treatment varies by underlying cause:

**Feline Idiopathic Cystitis (FIC):**
- Stress reduction is primary treatment
- Increased water intake and wet food transition
- Analgesics for pain (buprenorphine, meloxicam)
- Environmental enrichment
- Amitriptyline or gabapentin for chronic/recurrent cases
- Most uncomplicated FIC resolves within 5–7 days

**Urinary Stones (Uroliths):**
- Struvite stones: Often dissolve with prescription diet over 2–3 months
- Calcium oxalate stones: Require surgical removal (do not dissolve)
- Long-term prescription urinary diet to prevent recurrence

**Urethral Obstruction (Emergency Treatment):**
- IV fluids and stabilization
- Urethral catheterization under sedation/anesthesia
- Hospitalization for monitoring
- Perineal urethrostomy (PU surgery) for recurrent obstructions

**Bacterial Urinary Tract Infection (UTI):**
- Antibiotics based on urine culture and sensitivity
- Less common in cats than dogs — culture confirmation essential before treating

## Emergency Protocol: Urethral Obstruction
**A blocked cat is a life-threatening emergency — act immediately:**

Signs of complete blockage:
- No urine produced despite repeated straining attempts
- Crying in pain
- Distended, firm, painful abdomen
- Lethargy, vomiting, collapse

**Action:** Go to an emergency veterinary hospital immediately — do not wait. Without treatment, a blocked cat can die within 24–48 hours from electrolyte imbalances (hyperkalemia) causing cardiac arrest.

## Breeds Commonly Affected
- Persian (calcium oxalate stones)
- Himalayan (urinary stones; related to Persian ancestry)
- All breeds affected by FIC; no strong breed predisposition for idiopathic cystitis
- Male cats of any breed significantly more vulnerable to obstruction
