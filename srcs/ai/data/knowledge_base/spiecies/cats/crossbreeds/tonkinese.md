# Tonkinese (Siamese × Burmese)

## Overview
The Tonkinese was developed in Canada and the United States during the 1960s–1970s by crossing Siamese and Burmese cats to create a breed that balanced the extremes of both parents — less vocal and intense than the Siamese, more active and social than the Burmese. The breed produces three distinct coat pattern types depending on genetic inheritance, with the "mink" pattern (intermediate between pointed and solid) being most prized. They are recognized by TICA and CFA as a distinct breed.

## Visual Identification Markers

### Definitive Physical Traits
- **Body Type**: Medium, between the slender Siamese and the more compact Burmese — often described as "modified wedge" (intermediate between Oriental and cobby)
- **Coat**: Short, fine, silky with a distinctive mink-like sheen; minimal shedding
- **Pattern Types** (determined by color genetics):
  - **Mink**: Intermediate coloring — moderate contrast between points and body (most distinctive Tonkinese pattern)
  - **Pointed**: Higher contrast, similar to Siamese (one parent Siamese gene)
  - **Solid**: Low contrast, body and points nearly the same color (inherited from Burmese side)
- **Eyes**: Most distinctive feature — **aqua/teal** color in mink pattern; blue in pointed; yellow-green in solid
- **Muscle Tone**: Surprisingly heavy and muscular for their size ("a brick wrapped in silk" description)

### Size
- **Medium** (6–12 lbs); females notably lighter than males
- Denser and heavier than appearance suggests

## Detection Heuristics for Vision AI

**High Likelihood Indicators:**
1. **Siamese probability: 0.35-0.55** AND **Burmese probability: 0.35-0.55**
2. **Aqua or teal eye color** (near-definitive when combined with mink coat pattern — neither pure Siamese nor pure Burmese has this eye color)
3. Intermediate body type: moderate muscle, not extreme Oriental slender nor extreme cobby
4. Semi-pointed mink coat (moderate contrast, not high contrast of Siamese nor absent contrast of Burmese)
5. Fine, silky, close-lying short coat with satiny sheen

**Trait Conflicts Suggesting Cross-Breed:**
- Siamese-like coloring + Burmese compact build
- Aqua/teal eye color with mink coat (unique to Tonkinese — not present in either parent breed)
- Intermediate point intensity (not fully pointed, not fully solid)

## Generation Types

### Mink Pattern (Siamese cs gene × Burmese cb gene = cs/cb heterozygous)
- The Tonkinese-specific pattern — only produced when breeding a cs carrier with a cb carrier
- Moderate color-point contrast; aqua eyes
- Most "typical" Tonkinese appearance
- When mink × mink bred: statistically 25% pointed, 50% mink, 25% solid offspring

### Pointed Pattern (cs/cs homozygous)
- Inherited Siamese color pattern
- High contrast pointing, blue eyes
- Recognized in Tonkinese programs but resembles Siamese

### Solid Pattern (cb/cb homozygous)
- Inherited Burmese pattern
- Low contrast, uniform coloring
- Yellow-green eyes; resembles Burmese

## Temperament (Inherited Traits)

### From Siamese:
- Vocality (present but moderated compared to pure Siamese)
- High intelligence and curiosity
- People-orientation and social need
- Playfulness and athleticism

### From Burmese:
- Moderating of extreme Siamese personality
- Affection without overwhelming neediness
- Adaptability to different household types
- Dog-like loyalty and companionship

### Combined Traits:
- **Social**: Enjoys people, other pets, and activity; not suited to isolation
- **Playful**: Highly interactive and entertaining; clown-like behavior
- **Vocal but Moderate**: Communicative without the Siamese's insistent yowling
- **Intelligent**: Quick learners; enjoy puzzle toys, fetch, and clicker training
- **Affectionate**: Seeks contact and companionship; not a lap ornament — wants to participate

## Health Considerations

### Common to Both Parents:
- **Amyloidosis**: Inherited from Siamese lineage; abnormal protein deposits in organs
- **Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM)**: Risk inherited from both parent breeds; cardiac screening advisable
- **Dental Disease**: Prone to periodontal disease; regular dental care essential

### Siamese-Inherited:
- **Mediastinal Lymphoma**: Elevated cancer risk shared with Siamese
- **Progressive Retinal Atrophy (PRA)**: From Siamese genetic contribution

### Burmese-Inherited:
- **Hypokalaemia**: Periodic muscle weakness from low blood potassium; genetic predisposition in Burmese lines
- **Flat-Chested Kitten Syndrome (FCKS)**: Neonatal chest deformity; rare but documented in Burmese-related breeds

### Hybrid Notes:
- Generally considered healthier than either pure parent breed
- Some reduction in extreme Siamese health issues due to genetic mixing

## Care Requirements

### Exercise:
- **High energy**: Daily interactive play sessions required — wand toys, fetch, puzzle feeders
- Benefit from a feline companion of similar energy level

### Grooming:
- **Minimal**: Weekly brushing of the short coat; occasional bath if desired
- Ear cleaning monthly; dental care regular

### Social Needs:
- Should not be left alone for extended periods; pairs well with another cat
- Thrives in active households

### Training:
- Highly trainable; responsive to positive reinforcement
- Can learn leash walking, fetch, and complex tricks

## Nutrition Recommendations
- High-quality protein diet
- Moderate portions; body condition monitoring important (dense muscle can mask appropriate weight range)
- Fresh water always available; consider water fountain

## Common Misconceptions

❌ **"Tonkinese are just Siamese with different eyes"**
✅ Burmese contributes significant temperament moderation, body type differences, and the unique mink coat genetics

❌ **"Aqua eyes always mean Tonkinese"**
✅ Aqua eyes occur in other breeds; the combination with mink coat pattern and intermediate body type is the definitive indicator

❌ **"Tonkinese are easier than Siamese"**
✅ They're less intense, but still very social and active — they are not low-maintenance cats

## Lifespan
12–16 years

## Recommendations for Owners

**Best For:**
- Active households wanting an interactive, social cat
- Owners who found pure Siamese too demanding but want similar energy and intelligence
- Multi-pet households (pairs exceptionally well with other cats)

**Not Ideal For:**
- Owners wanting a quiet, independent cat
- Households where the cat is alone most of the day
- Those expecting minimal vocal communication
